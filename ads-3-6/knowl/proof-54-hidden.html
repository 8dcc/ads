Case 1: \(\lvert G\rvert < \infty\text{.}\) If \(a\) is a generator of \(G\) and \(\lvert G\rvert =n\text{,}\) define \(\phi:\mathbb{Z}_n \to G\) by \(\phi(k) = k a\) for all \(k \in \mathbb{Z}_n\text{.}\)

Since \(\langle a \rangle\) is finite, we can use the fact that the elements of \(\langle a \rangle\) are the first \(n\) nonnegative multiples of \(a\text{.}\) From this observation, we see that \(\phi\) is a surjection. A surjection between finite sets of the same cardinality must be a bijection. Finally, if \(p,q \in \mathbb{Z}_n\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{split} \phi(p)+\phi(q) &= p a + q a\\ &= (p+q)a \\ &= (p +_n q)a \quad \textrm{ see exercise 10}\\ & = \phi(p +_n q)\\ \end{split} \end{equation*}

Therefore \(\phi\) is an isomorphism.

Case 2: \(\lvert G\rvert =\infty\text{.}\) We will leave this case as an exercise.
