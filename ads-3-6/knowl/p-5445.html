A second way of describing a permutation is by means of cycles, which we will introduce first with an example. Consider \(f\in S_8\) defined using the now-familiar matrix notation:

\begin{equation*} f=\left( \begin{array}{cccccccc} 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 & 6 & 7 & 8 \\ 8 & 2 & 7 & 6 & 5 & 4 & 1 & 3 \\ \end{array} \right) \end{equation*}

Consider the images of 1 when \(f\) is applied repeatedly. The images \(f(1)\text{,}\) \(f(f(1))\text{,}\) \(f(f(f(1))), \ldots\) are \(8, 3, 7, 1, 8, 3, 7,\ldots\text{.}\) In FigureÂ 15.3.10(a), this situation is represented by a graph that with 1, 8, 3, and 7 and shows that the values that you get by repeatedly applying \(f\) cycle through those values. This is why we refer to this part of \(f\) as a cycle of length 4. Of course starting at 8, 3, or 7 also produces the same cycle with only the starting valued changing.
