We can generalize the previous example as follows: If \(n, m \geq 1\) and \(A\) is an \(m\times n\) matrix of 0’s and 1’s (elements of \(\mathbb{Z}_2\)), then \(\Phi :\mathbb{Z}_2{}^m\to \mathbb{Z}_2{}^n\) defined by
\begin{equation*} \Phi \left(a_1, a_2 , . . . , a _m \right) = \left(a_1, a_2 , . . . , a _m\right)A \end{equation*}
is a homomorphism. This is true because matrix multiplication is distributive over addition. The only new idea here is that computation is done in \(\mathbb{Z}_2\text{.}\) If \(a=\left(a_1, a_2 , . . . , a _m\right)\) and \(b=\left(b_1, b_2 , . . . , b _m\right)\text{,}\) \((a + b)A = a A + b A\) is true by basic matrix laws. Therefore, \(\Phi (a + b) = \Phi (a) + \Phi (b)\text{.}\)
