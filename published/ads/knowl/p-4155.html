Computing Modular Multiplicative Inverses. Unlike the nice neat formula for additive inverses mod \(n\text{,}\) multiplicative inverses can most easily computed by applying Bézout’s lemma. If \(a\) is an element of the group \(\mathbb{U}_n\text{,}\) then by definition \(\gcd(n,a)=1\text{,}\) and so there exist integers \(s\) and \(t\) such that \(1 = n s + a t\text{.}\) They can be computed with the Extended Euclidean Algorithm.
\begin{equation*} 1 = n s + a t \Rightarrow a t = 1 +(-s)n \Rightarrow a \times_n t =1 \end{equation*}
Since \(t\) might not be in \(\mathbb{U}_n\) you might need take the remainder after dividing it by \(n\text{.}\) Normally, that involves simply adding \(n\) to \(t\text{.}\)
