Note 12.4.7.

  1. The first step in the diagonalization process is the determination of the eigenvalues. The ordering of the eigenvalues is purely arbitrary. If we designate \(\lambda_1 = 4\) and \(\lambda_2=1\text{,}\) the columns of \(P\) would be interchanged and \(D\) would be \(\left( \begin{array}{cc} 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ \end{array} \right)\) (see Exercise 3b of this section). Nonetheless, the final outcome of the application to which we are applying the diagonalization process would be the same.

  2. If \(A\) is an \(n\times n\) matrix with distinct eigenvalues, then \(P\) is also an \(n\times n\) matrix whose columns \(P^{(1)}\text{,}\) \(P^{(2)}, \ldots\text{,}\) \(P^{(n)}\) are \(n\) linearly independent vectors.
