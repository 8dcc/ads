Once again there is only one free variable in the row reduction and so the dimension of the eigenspace will be one:

\begin{equation*} \left( \begin{array}{c} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ \end{array} \right)=\left( \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ x_3 \\ x_3 \\ \end{array} \right)= x_3\left( \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 1 \\ 1 \\ \end{array} \right) \end{equation*}

Hence, \(\left\{\left( \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 1 \\ 1 \\ \end{array} \right)\right\}\) is a basis for the eigenspace of \(\lambda_2= 3\text{.}\) This means that \(\lambda_2= 3\) produces only one column for \(P\text{.}\) Since we began with only two eigenvalues, we had hoped that \(\lambda_2= 3\) would produce a vector space of dimension two, or, in matrix terms, two linearly independent columns for \(P\text{.}\) Since \(A\) does not have three linearly independent eigenvectors \(A\) cannot be diagonalized.
