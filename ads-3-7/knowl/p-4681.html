The proof of this identity was an exercise in Section 5.4. The condition that \(D\) be a diagonal matrix is not necessary but when it is, the calculation on the right side is particularly easy to perform. Although the formal proof is done by induction, the reason why it is true is easily seen by writing out an example such as \(m=3\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{split} A^3 & = \left(P D P^{-1}\right)^3\\ & =\left(P D P^{-1}\right)\left(P D P^{-1}\right)\left(P D P^{-1}\right)\\ &= P D \left(P^{-1}P\right) D \left(P^{-1}P \right)D P^{-1}\quad\textrm{by associativity of matrix multiplication}\\ &= P D I D I D P^{-1}\\ &= P D D D P^{-1}\\ &= P D^3 P^{-1}\\ \end{split} \end{equation*}
